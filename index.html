Section One

The Fruit Basket
Intuition

Imagine a basket filled with mangos, bananas, oranges, and lemons. What is the most optimal composition of this basket? There is no one right answer , or is there? To understand the right composition, you must first understand your desires.

If you wish for a sweeter experience, mangos and bananas take precedence. If you seek balance, keep every fruit in equal measure. For something citrusy, increase the oranges and lemons. To optimize your experience, you must strike a balance between what you want versus what you are willing to give up.

Now , replace the fruits with businesses, and the basket with a portfolio. You have just intuitively grasped the essence of portfolio management theory.

The principle of optimal diversification is illustrated beautifully by Burton G. Malkiel's island economy parable. Consider an island with two businesses: a resort hotel that thrives in sunshine, and an umbrella manufacturer that flourishes in rain. An investor in only one faces significant risk. But invest in both , and the losses of one are cushioned by the gains of the other. This is diversification in its purest form.

The central question of this study follows naturally: while we all agree that diversification is good, which method of achieving it is actually better? Should you simply split capital equally across all assets, or should you apply mathematical optimization to find the very best combination?

Portfolio management has evolved from arbitrary decisions made by instinct to modern techniques employing advanced mathematics, data science, and even physics. Though the methodology has changed drastically, the underlying goal has remained the same: maximize returns for a given level of risk, or minimize risk for a given expected return.

This research tests traditional equal-weight heuristics against mean-variance optimized portfolios using a shared dataset of nine large-cap Indian equities, over a seven-year period spanning the pre-pandemic era, the COVID-19 crisis, and the subsequent recovery.

Section Two

The Shoulders
of Giants

Seven decades of financial economics research have shaped how we think about portfolios. The literature begins with a revolutionary insight from a young economist in 1952 and evolves into one of the most rich, contested, and practically relevant bodies of knowledge in finance.

Harry Markowitz
1927 – 2023
The father of Modern Portfolio Theory. His 1952 paper showed that investors should not evaluate assets in isolation, but in terms of their contribution to the entire portfolio's risk and return. He introduced mean-variance optimization and the concept of the efficient frontier.
Portfolio Selection , Journal of Finance, 1952
William F. Sharpe
1934 –
Transformed Markowitz's normative theory into a positive asset pricing model. The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) shows that only systematic risk (beta) is rewarded, since idiosyncratic risk can be diversified away. Also created the Sharpe Ratio , the most widely used risk-adjusted performance metric.
Capital Asset Prices , Journal of Finance, 1964
James Tobin
1918 – 2002
Extended Markowitz by introducing a risk-free asset into the investment universe. His two-fund separation theorem shows all investors should hold the same portfolio of risky assets (the tangency portfolio), differing only in how much of their wealth they put in the risk-free asset. This gave us the Capital Market Line.
Liquidity Preference as Behavior Toward Risk , 1958
Eugene F. Fama
1939 –
With James MacBeth, developed a landmark cross-sectional regression approach to test the CAPM. Their empirical work confirmed a positive relationship between beta and expected returns, but also opened the door to questioning whether beta alone explains all of asset pricing , eventually leading to the Fama-French Three-Factor Model.
Risk, Return & Equilibrium , Journal of Political Economy, 1973
DM
DeMiguel, Garlappi & Uppal
2009
In a landmark paper, they tested fourteen sophisticated optimization models against the naive 1/N equal-weight rule across multiple datasets. Their conclusion was striking: not a single optimized strategy consistently beat the simple equal-weight approach. Estimation error in returns and covariance matrices erodes all theoretical advantages of optimization.
Optimal vs. Naive Diversification , Review of Financial Studies, 2009
BG
Benjamin Graham
1894 – 1976
The father of value investing, whose opening quote frames the entire study. Graham was skeptical of elaborate mathematical models in investing, preferring fundamental analysis and a margin of safety. His intellectual legacy reminds us that no model, however elegant, is a substitute for good judgment and an understanding of business reality.
The Intelligent Investor , 1949 (and everything after)

The Mathematics

The study uses five core analytical techniques. Each builds on the previous, and together they form the complete picture.

Expected Return
E(Ri) = (1/T) ∑ Ri,t
The average historical monthly return of each asset, used to estimate future performance. Simple, but the foundation of everything that follows.
Covariance & Correlation
ρi,j = Cov(Ri, Rj) / (σi ⋅ σj)
Measures how assets move together. A correlation of +1 means they move in perfect lockstep. A correlation of -1 means they move in perfect opposition , the holy grail of diversification. Zero means no relationship. Real portfolios sit somewhere in between.
Sharpe Ratio
Sp = (E(Rp) - Rf) / σp
The most important single number in this study. It tells you how much return you earn per unit of total risk taken. A higher Sharpe ratio means you are being rewarded more efficiently for the risk you carry.
Jensen's Alpha
αp = Rp - [Rf + βp(Rm - Rf)]
Measures how much a portfolio earned above what the CAPM predicted it should earn given its beta. Positive alpha means the portfolio delivered more than the market implied it should , a sign of genuine outperformance.
Treynor Ratio
Tp = (Rp - Rf) / βp
Like the Sharpe ratio, but divides by systematic risk (beta) instead of total risk. More useful when comparing well-diversified portfolios, since it focuses only on the risk that cannot be diversified away.

The traditional portfolio is disarmingly simple: every stock gets exactly 11.11% of the capital. No calculations, no forecasts, no assumptions required. It maximizes diversification by number of holdings and provides a clean baseline for comparison.

The optimized portfolio, by contrast, was constructed using the Markowitz mean-variance framework. The model evaluates thousands of possible weight combinations, seeking the single portfolio with the highest Sharpe ratio , the best return per unit of risk. The result is striking and, at first glance, counterintuitive.

Bharti Airtel (BRTI) receives over 52% of the entire portfolio. Reliance, HDFC Bank, Hindustan Unilever, and L&T receive nothing at all. Why? Because the optimizer found that Airtel had low correlation with the other assets , it moves independently. In a telecom sector powered by strong recurring cashflows and policy tailwinds, this independence makes it extraordinarily valuable to the portfolio's overall efficiency. The optimizer did not care about sector concentration. It cared about mathematical efficiency.

This is exactly the difference between the intuitive, human approach to allocation and the purely mathematical one. One feels right; the other is right , at least by the math.

Hypothesis Testing
H1
Active and passive strategies differ in their impact on portfolio returns
Validated
The optimized portfolio delivered cumulative returns of ~20% versus ~15% for the equal-weighted portfolio , a difference of 500 basis points over the study period. Portfolio construction strategy matters enormously, even when the underlying assets are identical.
H2
Diversification level and asset correlation significantly influence portfolio risk
Validated
Correlation clusters (HDBK-LART at 0.61, LART-TITN at 0.57) and outliers (HLL near zero with most assets) directly shaped portfolio risk. The optimized portfolio's lower beta (0.91 vs 1.01) confirmed that strategic correlation management reduces systematic exposure.
H3
Systematic and unsystematic risk significantly affect risk-adjusted returns
Validated
The optimized portfolio recorded higher Sharpe (0.409), higher Treynor (0.024), and positive Jensen's Alpha (0.007). The equal-weighted portfolio showed near-negative alpha, implying its returns did not exceed what its systematic risk exposure (beta) would predict.
H4
Mean-variance optimization leads to higher portfolio efficiency
Validated
The optimized portfolio sits on the efficient frontier at the maximum Sharpe ratio point (0.409). Asset allocation departed significantly from equal weighting, concentrating capital in assets with the most favorable return-risk-correlation profiles, particularly Bharti Airtel's outsized allocation.
H5
Modern portfolio construction generates superior risk-adjusted performance
Validated
The optimized strategy outperformed the traditional approach across every measured dimension: absolute returns, Sharpe ratio, Treynor ratio, Jensen's Alpha, and cumulative performance. The theoretical advantages of modern portfolio theory were preserved , at least within this controlled empirical framework.

Section Seven

Discussion
& Limitations

The results provide empirical support for Modern Portfolio Theory. But this section does something more intellectually honest and interesting: it interrogates those results, questions the assumptions that produced them, and acknowledges where the model departs from reality.

As Charlie Munger advised: Invert, always invert. Instead of only asking why the optimized portfolio did well, the study asks why the traditional portfolio lagged , and the answer is illuminating. Broad-based equal allocation forces capital into low-performing and high-volatility assets that a disciplined optimizer would avoid. There is no allocation discipline, no correlation awareness, and no mathematical basis for the weights. What you gain in simplicity, you lose in efficiency.

01
Be Data-Driven
Portfolio design should be data-driven, because data helps curtail the dangerous "feeling" of entropy by making the entire process systematic and replicable.
02
Think in Risk-Adjusted Terms
A higher nominal return that comes with dramatically higher risk is not necessarily better. Investors must internalize risk-adjusted thinking , the Sharpe ratio, not the return, is the scorecard.
03
Diversify with Intent
Holding more stocks is not the same as being well-diversified. True diversification means holding assets that respond differently to the same market events , correlation is the key, not count.
04
Even Passive Investors Benefit
Though this study critiques pure passive approaches, a hybrid model , periodic optimization of a largely passive portfolio , would capture much of the efficiency gain without the full operational burden of active management.
Limitations of the Study
LIMIT 01
The risk-free rate was set at 5% p.a. rather than the more realistic 6.5%–7.5% (a 10-year G-Sec yield). This simplified modelling choice affects all ratio calculations, though the academic conclusions remain directionally valid.
LIMIT 02
The study uses a synthetic benchmark constructed from the nine selected stocks. This creates circularity , any portfolio built from these stocks will naturally be highly correlated with this benchmark. Using NIFTY 50 would introduce a different kind of mismatch.
LIMIT 03
The study assumes frictionless trading , no transaction costs, no rebalancing costs, no taxes. This makes the results more academic than directly actionable in real portfolio management.
LIMIT 04
The assumption that all passive portfolios follow an equal-weight strategy is a simplification. Real-world passive strategies are typically market-cap weighted, which produces very different results than equal-weighting.
LIMIT 05
The MPT itself rests on assumptions the paper openly critiques: perfect rationality, normally distributed returns, perfect liquidity. Real markets violate all of these, particularly during stress events. Fat tails in the return distributions confirm this empirically.

The Modern Portfolio Theory, for all its elegance, banks on some "absurd" assumptions. The most potent example: it equates risk with volatility. But a more grounded definition of risk is simply the potential loss of invested capital , and volatility is just price fluctuation. Equating the two, as the paper notes, is like comparing apples to oranges. These limitations do not invalidate the study; they contextualize it honestly.

Section Eight

Conclusion &
Recommendations

After seven years of data, nine large-cap Indian stocks, and two fundamentally different approaches to capital allocation, the evidence is clear. Portfolio optimization , when rigorously applied , delivers meaningfully superior risk-adjusted performance compared to naive diversification. The theoretical advantages of Modern Portfolio Theory were empirically preserved within this study's controlled framework.

↑
The optimized portfolio achieved a Sharpe ratio of 0.409 vs 0.335 , superior returns per unit of total risk, a difference of approximately 22%.
↓
The optimized portfolio carried lower systematic risk (beta 0.91 vs 1.01), demonstrating that better returns do not always require more risk.
↑
Cumulative returns of approximately 20% vs 15% over the study period , a difference of 500 basis points, purely from the construction methodology.
+
Positive Jensen's Alpha of 0.007 for the optimized portfolio, versus near-negative alpha for the equal-weighted approach , confirming genuine outperformance above the CAPM benchmark.
◆
Correlation analysis proved that diversification benefits depend on the interaction between assets, not simply the number of assets held. Holding more does not automatically mean being better protected.
Recommendations

For individual investors: incorporate correlation analysis and diversification structure into portfolio decisions, rather than relying solely on equal allocation. The number of holdings is less important than how those holdings interact with each other.

For portfolio managers: consider optimization tools grounded in Modern Portfolio Theory when designing diversified portfolios. The model is imperfect, but it is still better than intuition alone , particularly at scale.

For future research: expand the asset universe to multiple asset classes and sectors. Incorporate a proper market benchmark like NIFTY 50 or BSE 500. Extend the time horizon to cover multiple complete market cycles, giving a more robust picture of how optimization holds up across very different economic environments.

And perhaps the most practical recommendation of all: portfolio design must integrate both quantitative modelling and qualitative judgment. Mathematics can tell you the optimal allocation given your inputs. What it cannot tell you is whether your inputs are right , and that is where human judgment, sector knowledge, and macroeconomic awareness become irreplaceable.
