Section One

The Fruit Basket
Intuition

Imagine a basket filled with mangos, bananas, oranges, and lemons. What is the most "optimal" composition of this basket? There is no single right answer. To understand the right composition, you must first understand your desires. If you wish for sweetness, mangos and bananas take precedence. If you seek balance, keep every fruit in equal measure. To "optimise" your experience, you must strike a balance between what you want versus what you are willing to give up.

Now replace the fruits with businesses, and "basket" with "portfolio" — and you will have intuitively developed some understanding of portfolio management theory.

Section Two

Standing on
Shoulders of Giants

Seven decades of financial economics research have shaped how we think about portfolios. The literature begins with a revolutionary insight from a young economist in 1952 and evolves into one of the most contested bodies of knowledge in finance.

Harry Markowitz
1927 — 2023
Father of Modern Portfolio Theory. His 1952 paper showed that investors should not evaluate assets in isolation, but by their contribution to the entire portfolio's risk and return. He introduced mean-variance optimisation and the efficient frontier.
Portfolio Selection — Journal of Finance, 1952
William F. Sharpe
1934 —
Transformed Markowitz's normative theory into CAPM, showing only systematic risk (beta) is rewarded. Created the Sharpe Ratio — the most widely used risk-adjusted performance metric in existence.
Capital Asset Prices — Journal of Finance, 1964
JT
James Tobin
1918 — 2002
Extended Markowitz by introducing a risk-free asset. His two-fund separation theorem shows all investors should hold the same tangency portfolio of risky assets, differing only in how much wealth they allocate to the risk-free rate.
Liquidity Preference as Behavior Toward Risk — 1958
EF
Eugene F. Fama
1939 —
With James MacBeth, developed a landmark cross-sectional regression to test CAPM. Their 1973 empirical work confirmed a positive relationship between beta and expected returns, opening the door to questioning whether beta alone explains all of asset pricing.
Risk, Return and Equilibrium — Journal of Political Economy, 1973
DG
DeMiguel, Garlappi & Uppal
2009
Tested 14 sophisticated optimisation models against the naive 1/N equal-weight rule across multiple datasets. Conclusion: not a single optimised strategy consistently beat simple equal-weight. Estimation error erodes all theoretical advantages.
Optimal vs. Naive Diversification — Review of Financial Studies, 2009
BG
Benjamin Graham
1894 — 1976
The father of value investing whose opening quote frames this entire study. Graham was deeply sceptical of elaborate mathematical models, preferring fundamental analysis and a margin of safety. No model, however elegant, replaces good judgment.
The Intelligent Investor — 1949

The Five Formulas
Expected Return
E(Ri) = (1/T) Σ Ri,t
The average historical monthly return of each asset. Simple, but the foundation of everything that follows. It asks: on average, how did this asset perform?
Covariance & Correlation
ρi,j = Cov(Ri, Rj) / (σi · σj)
Measures how assets move together. A correlation of +1 means perfect lockstep. A correlation of −1 means perfect opposition — the holy grail of diversification. Real portfolios sit somewhere in between.
Sharpe Ratio
Sp = (E(Rp) − Rf) / σp
The most important single number in this study. How much return do you earn per unit of total risk? Higher is better. An annualised Sharpe above 1.0 is considered excellent in practice.
Jensen's Alpha
αp = Rp − [Rf + βp(Rm − Rf)]
How much did the portfolio earn above what CAPM predicted given its beta? Positive alpha means genuine outperformance — the portfolio delivered more than the market implied it should.
Treynor Ratio
Tp = (Rp − Rf) / βp
Like the Sharpe Ratio, but divides by systematic risk (beta) instead of total risk. Useful for well-diversified portfolios, since it focuses only on the market risk that cannot be diversified away.

Section Eight

Discussion
& Limitations

The results provide empirical support for Modern Portfolio Theory. But this section does something more intellectually honest: it interrogates those results, questions the assumptions that produced them, and acknowledges where the model departs from reality.

As Charlie Munger advised: Invert, always invert. Instead of only asking why the optimised portfolio did well, the study asks why the traditional portfolio lagged. The answer: broad equal allocation forces capital into low-performing and high-volatility assets that a disciplined optimiser would avoid.

01
Be Data-Driven
Portfolio design should be data-driven. Data helps curtail the dangerous "feeling" of entropy by making the entire process systematic and replicable.
02
Think in Risk-Adjusted Terms
A higher nominal return with dramatically higher risk is not necessarily better. Investors must internalise risk-adjusted thinking — the Sharpe ratio, not the raw return, is the real scorecard.
03
Diversify with Intent
Holding more stocks is not the same as being well-diversified. True diversification means holding assets that respond differently to the same events — correlation is the key, not count.
04
Passive Investors Benefit Too
A hybrid model — periodic optimisation of a largely passive portfolio — would capture much of the efficiency gain without the full operational burden of active management.
Limitations of the Study
Limit 01
The risk-free rate was fixed at 6.5% p.a. The actual 10-year G-Sec yield fluctuated between 6.5% and 7.5% over the study period, introducing a degree of approximation into all ratio calculations.
Limit 02
The synthetic benchmark creates circularity — any portfolio built from these nine stocks will naturally be highly correlated with this benchmark. Using NIFTY 50 would introduce a different structural mismatch given the restricted asset universe.
Limit 03
The study assumes frictionless trading: no transaction costs, no rebalancing costs, no taxes. This makes the results more academic than directly actionable in real portfolio management.
Limit 04
The assumption that all passive portfolios follow an equal-weight strategy is a simplification. Real-world passive strategies are typically market-capitalisation weighted, producing very different results.
Limit 05
MPT's greatest drawback: equating risk with volatility. A more grounded definition of risk is the potential loss of invested capital, while volatility is just price fluctuation. Fat tails in the return distributions confirm this empirically.

Section Nine

Conclusion &
Recommendations

After seven years of data, nine large-cap Indian stocks, and two fundamentally different approaches to capital allocation, the evidence is clear. Portfolio optimisation — when rigorously applied — delivers meaningfully superior risk-adjusted performance compared to naive diversification.

+
Monthly Sharpe ratio of 0.330 vs 0.229 — superior risk-adjusted returns, a difference of approximately 44%.
+
Annualised Sharpe of 1.143 vs 0.793, and Treynor of 1.91% vs 1.12% — consistent outperformance across every efficiency metric.
+
Cumulative returns of approximately ~500% vs ~270% — a difference of roughly 23,000 basis points, purely from the construction methodology.
+
Positive Jensen's Alpha of +0.71% for the optimised portfolio versus 0.00% for the equal-weighted approach — confirming genuine outperformance above the CAPM benchmark.
+
Correlation analysis proved that diversification benefits depend on how assets interact, not simply on how many assets are held.

For individual investors: incorporate correlation analysis and diversification structure into portfolio decisions, rather than relying solely on equal allocation. The number of holdings is less important than how those holdings interact.

For portfolio managers: consider optimisation tools grounded in Modern Portfolio Theory. The model is imperfect, but it is still better than intuition alone — particularly at scale.

And the most practical recommendation of all: portfolio design must integrate both quantitative modelling and qualitative judgment. Mathematics can tell you the optimal allocation given your inputs. What it cannot tell you is whether your inputs are right — and that is where human judgment, sector knowledge, and macroeconomic awareness become irreplaceable.
